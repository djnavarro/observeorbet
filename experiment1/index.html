This is an experiment looking at how people make choices in an uncertain world. In particular, we're interested in how people seek out the information they need to make good choices.

Suppose you want to buy a car. Before you buy anything, you'll probably try to find out how much money various different cars cost, and what you get for your money. This time spent collecting information doesn't provide you with any direct benefit: all it does is help you make a better choice when you do buy a car. And it comes at a cost: time spent researching cars is time you can't spend making money or doing things that you find fun. Because of this, one of the things you need to do is figure out when to do your research into car prices, and how much time you should spend on it.

Buying a car is a complex decision: our experiment is a LOT simpler than that. It takes the form of a simple "guessing game", which we'll now describe.


This is a blox machine.



It has two lights. The one on the left is blue, and the one on the right is red. Only one light can flash at a time:







The way a blox machine works is that the lights turn on and off in an irregular way, but with a bias. Some blox machines are biased to turn on the blue light more often, and others are biased to turn the red light more often.

But, there is a catch: the bias sometimes changes! A blox that used to be biased towards red can sometimes change its bias to blue, and vice versa. This doesn't happen all the time, but you should expect to see it happen a few times during this task

Here's how the game works. You'll see an interface that looks like this, showing a blox machine and three buttons you can press:





The game lasts for 50 "actions" (button presses), and your goal is to get as many points as possible in that time. The number of points you get depends heavily on what actions you take.

If you click the OBSERVE button, you won't get any points for that action. Instead, you get to observe the blox machine at work: one of the two lights will flash, allowing you to help figure out if the machine is biased toward red or blue.

Alternatively, you could choose to GUESS RED or GUESS BLUE. Whenever you guess, one of the lights does in fact turn on, but you won't be shown which one it was. At the end of the game, you'll find out how well you did: you get 1 point for every correct guess, but you lose 1 point for every incorrect guess. So your goal is to make as many correct guesses as possible without making too many incorrect guesses.


In order to get paid for the HIT, you'll be asked to play the game 5 times. Every game has a new blox machine: if the last one had a bias to prefer red, that doesn't mean the next one will be biased red too. In order to help you learn to play the game effectively, at the end of each game you'll get detailed feedback showing you what really happened on all 50 actions: you'll see what action you took, what the machine did, and whether you got a point (or lost one) on that action.
